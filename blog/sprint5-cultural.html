Reflecting on Reflections

“Since everything is a reflection of our minds, everything can be changed by our minds.” - Buddha

11 December 2020

What are three new things you have learned about yourself and your ego as a result of the core learning?

  • When I come up against a challenge, an initial response is often to find the easiest solution. Once realised, this can be harnessed for good. Someone once said laziness is the father of innovation. So yes, look for an easy out, a quick solution, something innovative, but most importantly, understand it, and make sure it bloody well works.

  • It can be easy to beat yourself up when you can’t solve a problem. Sometimes you just hit a wall and your mind goes blank. This happens, and it happens to everyone. But by believing in Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset, you can attack problems with the belief that if you keep pushing and keep trying, you will keep learning and developing, until soon enough, that problem seems like the simplest of issues.

  • Listening is hard. This was quite surprising. My girlfriend likes to tell me that I have selective listening. I never believed her until I tried to listen to everything someone had to say in two minutes. Having realised this after my first round of listening and looping, I was far more attentive in my second round. I look forward to doing more in bootcamp!

What are the roles of values, empathy and self awareness in learning and programming?

With these attributes in mind, we can develop a holistic-of-sorts approach to learning and programming. By understanding that we are all different, we all learn at different rates, and we all have different perspectives, we can harness the knowledge and help of those around us, and work as a team to aid in our own development. Taking in to account our own personal values, and a self awareness of our strengths and limitations, we can identify where and how we need to focus our time and energy, to help us learn and become better programmers/

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?

Its relevance. It is very easy to pooh-pooh this kind of learning. “I already know how to deal with people”,”I’ve worked in a professional capacity before”,”I know how to interview for a job and not look like a complete recloose”. These things are easy to say, but it’s harder to understand that we can always learn more about ourselves and those around us. Our development as humans is never ending, we are not computers built to do exactly as a function requires, but much more complex creatures, and it's worth spending time exploring that.

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?

Reflection. It’s always an easy one to skip. You finish a task and you’re eager to move on to the next one. I am still very much guilty of this. But It takes reflection, such as typing that last sentence out, to really understand. So have a break, have a kit-kat, and reflect on what you’ve just done.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development bootcamp course?

The cliche programmer sits in a dark room, ideally a windowless basement, of an office building and has no social skills whatsoever. In 2020, this is most certainly not the case. There is nothing wrong with being introverted and working autonomously, in many cases, that works very well. But in order to work as part of a team, we need to be sociable and understanding. This avoids conflict, and aids in effective communication, which of course leads to great work.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practicing programming instead? Justify your answer.

Absolutely not. As I have highlighted above, I have learnt a lot from the core side of this course. I look forward to more in bootcamp!
